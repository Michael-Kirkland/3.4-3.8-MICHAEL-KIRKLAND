Iran War

A Conflict of Escalation, Endurance, and Strategy

This is not just a war of bombs and borders. It is a strategic contest over who can absorb pain, impose costs, and control escalation.

Iran, the United States, and Israel are all playing different games — and each side believes time favors them in different ways.

The Iran war is often described in military terms, but the deeper logic is strategic. One side seeks to dominate through superior technology, precision strikes, and leadership decapitation. The other seeks to survive, retaliate, and make the price of victory too high. In game theory terms, this is not simply a fight over territory. It is a contest over incentives, signaling, credibility, and who can endure a longer and more expensive conflict. Recent reporting also describes Iran’s strategy in terms of endurance and economic disruption rather than conventional battlefield victory.

A digital outcome can affect different social groups in different ways. Social groupings can be looked at in terms of ethnicity, age, gender, religious beliefs, socio-economic status, and more. You need to ensure your digital outcome avoids offending anyone, mis-representing a social group or stereotyping people. Your outcome should appeal to all social groups, if possible.

Are all cultures treated fairly by your outcome? If you have used other languages have you ensured they are used appropriately and using the correct spelling? If you are using imagery from a minority culture, are you doing so respectfully? Have you shown or said anything that is offensive in some cultures? Obtaining feedback from people of different ethnic backgrounds to yourself often provides unexpected insights.

Does your outcome obey all relevant laws? These might include the Privacy Act, the Copyright Act, or Harmful Digital Communications laws. For example, have you made sure your digital outcome doesn’t make false allegations, denigrate a person’s ethnic origin or sexual orientation, contains no private information and contains nothing intellectual property that you don’t have permission to include?

Is it moral? Is it right? As designers and developers, it’s important that our products don’t cause harm to anyone. Think about whether your digital outcome exploits users, encourages irresponsible choices, is violent or pornographic.

Intellectual property refers to the ownership rights that people have over items, designs or ideas that they create. It can apply to a musical score, an idea, a painting or photo, code snippets, choreography, writing, and much more. The parts used to make up your digital outcomes may come from a variety of different places - you need to ensure you aren’t breaching anyone’s intellectual property rights by using their work in your digital outcome. For example, if you used a photo from pixabay on your website and its copyright conditions are ‘Free for commercial use
No attribution required’, this means you are allowed to use it and don’t need to attribute the owner of the image on your website. It’s important to know what the following terms mean:

  • Attribution - Users of your work must credit your name
  • Non-commercial- Users may not make money from your work
  • No derivatives - Users may not remix your work
  • Share alike - Users must share your work under the same license
  • Public Domain - Anyone can use the work in any way

Watch this short video about Creative Commons Kiwi to learn more: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AeTlXtEOplA

Activity: Add to your notes, answering the following questions: Are there copyright considerations relevant to your outcome? If so, what did you do to ensure your outcome doesn’t breach copyright? Why is it important to respect the intellectual property rights of other people?

As digital designers, we often have access to other people’s data, images or personal information. There are strict laws governing the storage and use of personal information in NZ. You can read more about the 12 principles of NZ’s Privacy Act here.

Think about how you have kept other people’s personal information safe and confidential. If you are using images of people in your website, do you have their permission? If your database contains private addresses, are these kept hidden from other users?

Activity: Add to your notes, answering the following questions: Are there privacy implications relevant to your outcome? If so, what did you do to ensure your outcome maintains the privacy of others?

When we make a digital outcome we should ensure most users can access it. This includes people using different screen-sizes or devices, people with impaired vision or hearing, and any other group in our community that may have accessibility needs.

the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities, which New Zealand has ratified states that we must recognise "the importance of accessibility to the physical, social, economic and cultural environment, to health and education and to information and communication, in enabling persons with disabilities to fully enjoy all human rights and fundamental freedoms." (Be. Accessible)

Here’s a short video from NZ organisation Be.Accessible which explains why accessible design is important to our communities: https://www.youtube.com/watch?time_continue=1&v=RyqMjFgi5Vs

How do blind users access the internet? https://levelaccess.com/understanding-assistive-technology-how-does-a-blind-person-use-the-internet/

Some examples of ways you can ensure you are meeting accessibility in your product might be:

  • The buttons on your phone app are big enough for people with low fine-motor control of their fingers to press
  • Colour schemes on your website that allow your text and images to be seen by colour-blind people
  • A good site uses proper semantic markup to allow individuals to non-visually understand the page structure and hierarchy. This also enables individuals to navigate to section headings and document landmarks on the page.
  • The print on your pamphlet is large enough to be readable to people with poor eyesight
  • You used alt tags and image descriptions on your website
  • You included a way for users to change the language in your game

Activity: Add to your notes, answering the following questions: What did you do to ensure your outcome is suitable for people with accessibility needs? Why is this important?

In year 11 you may have learned about usability heuristics. These are ‘rules of thumb’ you can follow to make the use of your outcome easier for users. If you need a refresher or you didn’t learn about this last year, please make use of the information and activities available in the CS Fieldguide.

While designing your digital outcome you would have thought about what your users need to do and how to help them do this. For example you may have thought about how the user inputs text into a text box and you may have made this easy for them using prompts and a large, visible input box.

Activity: Add to your notes, answering the following questions: What did you do to make your outcome easier to use?

How well does your outcome do what it is supposed to do? What have you done to ensure it functions as intended? This could include how fast a website loads, whether there are bugs in your code, whether it works on all devices. It’s good to think about specific things when you are investigating the functionality of your digital outcome. For example, rather than just deciding it functions well, you can be more specific and say that the links work, or the error messages explain the problem clearly and ask the user to try again, or the database query displays the correct results and only these, or that the images are clear and in proportion.

Activity: Add to your notes, answering the following questions: What are the functions of your outcome (what things is it expected to do)? How well does it perform these functions? Justify your answer by saying how you tested this.

Aesthetics is all about the look and feel of your outcome. When you design a digital outcome it helps to be aware of design conventions for the type of product you are making and what you can do to ensure that it appeals to your target audience. It is good if you can recognise and explain what it is that makes an item attractive. You may be able to conduct surveys among your target audience to gather evidence about how well you have met the need to create an aesthetically pleasing product.
You may have ensured your outcome meets aesthetic requirements by using a harmonious colour scheme, designing balanced screen layouts, and using a particular font to support the style of your product.

Activity: Add to your notes, answering the following questions: What aspects of your outcome make it aesthetically pleasing? Are there any things that could have improved it’s aesthetic appeal?

Isn’t it annoying when we need to buy a new phone because the old one won’t run the latest and coolest apps? It’s not much use designing items that are no longer usable a year later (although the manufacturer and shopkeeper might disagree!)
It’s important to design products that can be kept up to date. If it’s software, you should arrange the code in such a way that changes and updates can easily be made by other programmers later on. If it’s a website you might want to set your menu up in such as way that you can easily add pages later on. Using current coding languages and conventions is another way of future-proofing your outcome.

Activity: Add to your notes, answering the following questions: What did you do to ensure your outcome will still be useful in future?

The most beautiful website in the world is no use if it doesn’t meet its purpose because your user can’t read it!

Think about whether your outcome meets its purpose and does what the user needs. Eg does it use language at a suitable reading level for your users? Does it appeal to the right demographic? Is it available on the devices that your target audience uses?

Activity: Add to your notes, answering the following questions: What aspects of your outcome do your end users care about the most? How did you meet their needs? How did you test whether you had met their needs?

Is your outcome free from anything that could potentially harm your users? For example, a 3D-printed item with small parts that could come loose is not safe around babies. A website should be free from any text that encourages users to do anything dangerous or unhealthy.

Activity: Add to your notes, answering the following questions: Are there health and safety implications relevant to your outcome? If so, what are they?
