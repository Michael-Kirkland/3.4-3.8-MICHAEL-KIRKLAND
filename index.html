Iran War

2026 | The West vs. The Islamic Republic

A Conflict of Escalation, Endurance, and Strategy

This is not just a war of bombs and borders. It is a strategic contest over who can absorb pain, impose costs, and control escalation.

On 28 February 2026, an Israeli airstrike on a fortified bunker in northeast Tehran killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei. The shockwaves are still spreading. What began nine months earlier as the Twelve-Day War of June 2025 — Israel and the United States striking Iranian nuclear and command sites — has since reopened on a wider front, drawn Hezbollah back into combat, closed the Strait of Hormuz, and pushed global oil markets past historic highs. This site is an attempt to make sense of it.

Mourners fill the streets of Tehran the morning after Khamenei's death; 1 March 2026

Iran, the United States, and Israel are all playing different games — and each side believes time favors them in different ways.

The Iran war is often described in military terms, but the deeper logic is strategic. One side seeks to dominate through superior technology, precision strikes, and leadership decapitation. The other seeks to survive, retaliate, and make the price of victory unbearable. In game-theory terms, this is not a fight over territory. It is a contest over incentives, signaling, credibility, and endurance — over who can outlast a longer, costlier, uglier conflict. The framing this site uses, drawn from Predictive History's analysis of asymmetric conflict, treats the war as a game whose rules each player is trying to bend in their own favor.
